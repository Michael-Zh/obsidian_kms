# [[Daily-Procrastination-Protocol]]

## Why This Works

Procrastination is not a willpower failure — it is a cortisol-driven escape response. The brain treats the blocked task as a threat and switches to avoidance mode. All conventional "just do it" techniques add pressure to an already overloaded system and make the loop worse.

This protocol works by systematically lowering the activation cost of starting, not by demanding more from the nervous system. Each step is a de-escalation, not a push.

Source insight: [[Identity-Based-Life-Philosophy]] — procrastination neuroscience section (ref: [[困扰我15年的拖延症，被一个反直觉的方法终结了！你的大脑在求救，希望你越早知道越好。|困扰我15年的拖延症]])

## The Five-Step Sequence

Use in order when feeling blocked. You do not always need all five — stop when you feel the resistance lift.

### Step 1 — Conflict Intervention (name your temptations)

Write a list of the things you are *not* supposed to do right now.

> Social media. Checking messages. Opening another tab. Reorganizing the desk. "Just one video."

**Why it works:** Naming the temptations externalizes them. The brain stops treating them as live escape options once they are written down and acknowledged. You are not suppressing them — you are making them visible, which reduces their pull.

**Time:** 2 minutes.

---

### Step 2 — Two-Minute Rule + 15-Minute Threshold

Commit to starting for exactly two minutes. If still going at 15 minutes, you are past the threshold — the nervous system has disengaged from threat mode and momentum is available.

**Why it works:** The activation cost of starting is the real barrier. "Work on this for 2 hours" is a threat. "Touch this for 2 minutes" is not. The 15-minute threshold is when cortisol levels drop enough that continuation becomes natural rather than forced.

**Rule:** Do not judge or evaluate the output during this window. The only job is to be present with the task.

---

### Step 3 — Garbage Version

Explicitly aim for the worst acceptable output. Name it before you start.

> "I am writing the worst possible first paragraph." "This plan does not need to make sense." "This is the draft no one will see."

**Why it works:** Perfectionism is often the hidden driver underneath procrastination — the task cannot be started because starting means potentially failing. Committing to garbage output removes the identity stake from the opening move. The Competence Freeze patch from _POS applies: *Ship Draft 1 immediately.*

**Especially effective for:** writing tasks, analytical work, any output that will be evaluated.

---

### Step 4 — Reverse To-Do (log what you already did)

Write down everything you have already completed — today, or in recent days relevant to this project.

> "Read three articles. Opened the file. Responded to that email. Had the conversation I had been avoiding."

**Why it works:** Chronic procrastinators operate with a depleted sense of agency. Logging prior completions restores the sense that you are a person who does things. It counteracts the "I never get anything done" narrative that feeds the escape loop.

**Note:** This is different from Step 1. Step 1 neutralizes temptations before starting. Step 4 restores agency mid-session when the momentum from Step 2/3 fades.

---

### Step 5 — Simulation Game Perspective

Mentally step outside yourself and observe the task as if you are playing a character in a game.

> "My character has this task. What is the next smallest action the character would take?"

**Why it works:** The cortisol response is identity-based — *I* am failing, *I* am stuck, *I* am exposed. The simulation perspective detaches the task from self-identity. You are not protecting your ego anymore; you are just moving a piece on a board. Especially effective when the task carries emotional weight (performance review, difficult email, creative work).

## Application by Task Type

| Task type | Most useful steps |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Creative / writing | Step 1 → Step 3 (garbage version) → Step 2 |
| Analytical / work output | Step 2 → Step 3 → Step 1 if distracted |
| Emotionally loaded (difficult conversation, review) | Step 5 → Step 2 |
| "I have been avoiding this for days" | Step 4 → Step 1 → Step 2 |
| Scattered / can't focus | Step 1 → Step 2 |
