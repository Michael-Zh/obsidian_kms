# Role of strategist
I work in travel industry, matrix organizations. We found issue that we are selling lots of tickets without any baggage attached. We are trying to improve by sourcing more fare family with baggage options. Team is fragmented. And things are not moving well. What's my role here as strategist?

**ChatGPT said:**

Given your situation, **your role as a strategist is to create clarity, alignment, and momentum** in an environment where the team is fragmented and decisions aren’t moving. Strategy here is not just about what to do, but how to get the organization to act together.

Below is a clear breakdown of what your role should look like in this context.

  

—

  

**Your Role as Strategist in This Situation**

**1. Diagnose the real problem — not just the symptom**

Selling many tickets without baggage is the _symptom_.  
Your job is to answer:

- Why is this happening?
- Is it due to product gaps (fare families)?
- System limitations?
- Sales behavior?
- Confusing UI or booking flow?
- Lack of incentive or training?
- Suppliers not offering the right bundles?
- Misalignment between commercial, product, and sourcing teams?

You create the **fact base** and identify the root causes so action has a direction.

  

**2. Create a clear strategic direction**

You define what “good” looks like for the organization. For example:

**Strategic Goal:**  
Increase “tickets with baggage” penetration from X% to Y% by improving offering, merchandising, and organizational alignment.

**Strategic Pillars:**

1. Product availability — source richer fare families with baggage.
2. Merchandising — ensure customers _see_ and understand baggage options.
3. Sales & market optimization — encourage selection of the right fare.
4. Organizational alignment — clarify ownership and processes.

Your role: **turn a vague problem into a structured, actionable direction.**

  

**3. Align stakeholders across the matrix organization**

In a matrix environment, the biggest enemy is confusion.

Your job is to:

- Define ownership (who decides? who executes? who supports?)
- Create cross-functional understanding (product, sourcing, revenue, ops, sales)
- Facilitate agreement on the roadmap and priorities
- Remove ambiguity

Essentially, **you create the glue** that the fragmented team is missing.

  

**4. Translate strategy into a concrete roadmap**

Teams know _what_ to do but not _how_ or _in what order_.  
You build an execution plan, such as:

- **Short term (0–3 months):** Source key airlines’ baggage-inclusive fare families; update merchandising in booking flow
- **Mid term (3–6 months):** Improve sales incentives & training; implement default recommendation logic
- **Long term (>6 months):** Optimize bundles, upsell logic, and supplier negotiations

Your role: **turn the strategy into a timeline with owners, milestones, and KPIs.**

  

**5. Create KPIs and tracking**

You define how success is measured:

- % of tickets sold with baggage
- Revenue per passenger
- Attach rate of ancillary products
- Conversion impact of fare family redesign

You turn the problem into **measurable progress**.

  

**6. Ensure momentum and remove obstacles**

Because things are “not moving well,” a strategist must:

- Identify blockers (technical, organizational, supplier)
- Escalate or mediate as needed
- Provide clarity and decision frameworks
- Keep everyone aligned to the goal

You become the **driver of movement**, not just the planner.

  

**7. Communicate the narrative**

A fragmented team often lacks a shared story.

You create clear communication:

- Why baggage matters (financial impact, customer experience)
- What the plan is
- How teams contribute
- What success looks like

You become the **voice of direction**.

  

**In short, your strategist role:**

**You diagnose the problem, create the strategy, align the matrix, build the roadmap, define KPIs, and ensure execution stays on track.**

More simply:

**You make the organization understand the problem, agree on the solution, and move together toward results.**
