# HR BP 1-1 Prep
*First meeting since joining — 7 months in. She wants to understand your experience in the role.*

---

## 1. What you're enjoying about the role

The role plays to your full range and suits how you think. Lead with this to open on a positive note.

> "What I find genuinely energising is that this role demands breadth — analytics, strategy, project and stakeholder management, all in a cross-cultural context. I'm someone who needs intellectual challenge and a bit of distance from pure execution, so it fits well."

Key themes to convey:
- Blends many competencies: analytics, strategic thinking, project management, stakeholder management
- Cross-cultural and multi-location complexity is engaging, not a burden
- The elevated vantage point (not day-to-day firefighting) suits how you process and work

---

## 2. Onboarding feedback — frame as a gift to future hires

**IT and entity split (Travis):**
> "The thing that cost the most time early on was IT access and navigating the Travis/HQ split. It wasn't always obvious which entity handled what. A clearer handoff guide for new Europe-based hires would save a lot of friction."

**Manager:**
> "On the knowledge side, I was lucky — J invested a lot of time in me and her documentation is excellent. That was a real asset."
*(Give credit clearly — HR BPs notice this.)*

**HR policy visibility:**
> "I also found it hard to track down policies and benefits, partly because of the split entity setup. A single source of truth for Europe-based employees would help."

**Mobile reimbursement (practical issue to raise):**
During hiring, TA mentioned monthly mobile reimbursement — this has not been fulfilled. HR ops says it's case-by-case. Frame as a process question:
> "During the hiring process I was told I'd receive a monthly mobile reimbursement. HR ops explained it's granted case-by-case — could you help me understand the criteria, or whether there's a path for me to be considered?"

---

## 3. Career development

The move from _doing analysis_ to _influencing decisions_ is a meaningful and articulate direction.

> "What excites me about where I want to go is being in the room where decisions get made, not just supporting the room. In an AI-accelerated world, the premium shifts from technical execution to judgment and responsibility — and that's the direction I want to develop in."

**Important context to raise:** In the current role there is no clear path forward. If open to new opportunities within the company, there are two possible directions to explore with her:

- **Path 1 — New market development:** Follow a similar track to what J is doing now, moving into developing new markets.
- **Path 2 — Regional strategist:** Support the regional head on strategy. Currently the regional head is the only person working on strategy at that level, so there's a natural opening to build out that function.

This is worth naming explicitly — HR BPs can only help you move if they know what you're open to. Framing it as "I've thought about where I could grow and here are two directions that feel aligned" shows initiative without sounding like you're trying to leave your current role.

---

## 4. Performance evaluation — don't skip this

Most practically important topic. After 7 months, KPIs haven't yet moved and it's unclear how the review will go. Surface this openly:

> "One thing I'd genuinely value your perspective on is how performance is typically evaluated for strategic roles here — especially when goals are inherently ambiguous at the start. I was told that creating clarity is itself a contribution, but I'd like to understand better how that translates into a formal review."

This signals that you care about doing well and gives her a chance to actually help you.

---

## 5. Cross-cultural dynamics — frame as your edge

Being Chinese, fluent in both languages and cultures, in a Europe-based role is a genuine advantage. Name it as such.

> "My background helps a lot here. I can navigate both the cultural tempo and the communication style at HQ in a way that I imagine is quite disorienting for colleagues without that context."

No need to elaborate on all the specifics (directness, speed, documentation gaps) unless she asks — the key is to signal that you *see* the dynamics and can work with them. That's a value-add.

---

## 6. Manager situation — handle carefully

Things have improved significantly. Since it's largely resolved, avoid raising it as a complaint. If she asks broad questions about your experience, acknowledge it briefly:

> "There was a rough patch early on in terms of our working style, but we've figured out how to collaborate well."

Honest, mature, and forward-looking — without putting the manager under a spotlight unnecessarily.

---

## Closing move

Make the relationship ongoing. Ask how she prefers to stay in contact and whether there's a good cadence for check-ins. You want to leave as someone she'll proactively think of, not just a box she ticked.
