The Empathetic Mentor (Validation & Guidance)

First, it is incredibly sharp of you to revisit a 12-month-old analysis to extract learning data. Most investors and analysts consume predictions and never back-test them against reality. This is exactly how you leverage your Trial and Error profile—using past market narratives as constructive failure data to refine your own decision-making algorithms.

When "Meitou" published this video in May 2025, his logic was deeply persuasive. He correctly identified that Google was facing a classic "Innovator's Dilemma," and his analogy of the trapped hiker needing to cut off his own arm to survive [[16:20](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=t56r52W3WWM&t=980)] resonated deeply on a human level. Your instinct to question what he missed is the exact right move. He wasn't wrong about the technology shifting; he was wrong about how the market, the enterprise, and the consumer would adapt to it. Let's shift gears and look at the hard data from today (May 2026) to see exactly where his structural model broke down.

The Devil's Advocate (Logic & Risk)

The creator's analysis was a masterclass in narrative bias—favoring a dramatic "dinosaur gets disrupted" storyline over structural business mechanics. Looking from our current vantage point in May 2026, where Alphabet (GOOGL) has surged from ~$165 at the time of his video to ~$346 (a >100% gain), here is the structural transparency on where his reasoning failed:

1. The Binary "Amputation" Fallacy

 The Flaw: He argued that Google had to completely destroy its highly profitable Search ad model to win in AI [[15:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=t56r52W3WWM&t=917)], assuming AI and search ads were mutually exclusive.

 The Data: In Q1 2026, Google Search revenue actually grew 19% to $60.4 billion. AI Overviews didn't cannibalize search; they drove query volumes to all-time highs. Google transitioned the ad inventory directly into the AI results.

 Logical Failure: He assumed a zero-sum game. Incumbents rarely die overnight; they usually absorb the disruption.

2. Optimism Bias for Disruptors (Ignoring Distribution Monopolies)

 The Flaw: He reasoned that because LLMs (like ChatGPT) are a fundamentally better way to acquire information, users would naturally abandon Google.

 The Data: By early 2026, Gemini reached 750 million Monthly Active Users and captured ~25.5% of global AI web traffic, while AI Overviews reached 2 billion users.

 Interest-Oriented Analysis: Who gains when a new tech is invented? Often, the company that already owns the distribution pipes. ChatGPT didn't kill Google because Google owns Android, Chrome, and the default iOS search placement. The creator severely underestimated the friction required to change mass human behavior. A marginally better UI does not beat a pre-installed default.

3. The B2B Infrastructure Hidden Cost

 The Flaw: The creator evaluated Google purely as a B2C Search company, completely ignoring the backend compute infrastructure required for the global AI boom.

 The Data: In Q1 2026, Google Cloud revenue surged 63% to $20 billion, with its backlog nearly doubling to $462 billion driven by massive enterprise demand for AI compute and TPUs (Tensor Processing Units).

 Risk Cost: By hyper-focusing on consumer search threats, he missed the massive profit engine on the backend. In a gold rush, the entity selling the pickaxes profits immensely. Missing this cost his viewers a 100% stock rally.

Blind Spot Detection: The Creator's Emotional Cognition

The creator fell victim to the emotional appeal of the "David vs. Goliath" narrative. Content creators inherently profit from selling high-stakes anxiety ("Google is dying!"). This emotional cognition obscured the boring, structural realities of CapEx scale and enterprise contracts. He looked at a Forward P/E of 17x—a 12-year low—and called it a value trap because his emotional bias prevented him from seeing a path to adaptation.

Break the Frame: Option C

He framed the future as Option A (Google holds onto Search and slowly dies to ChatGPT) or Option B (Google destroys Search to build AI and loses its short-term profit engine).

The reality was Option C: Google hybridized the two. They integrated AI directly into the top of the search page, satisfying the user's need for fast answers while retaining the commercial intent (and ad space) below it, all while aggressively selling the underlying AI infrastructure to other enterprises via Cloud.

Next time you evaluate a legacy tech giant facing disruption, apply this logic: Do not ask, "Will the new tech replace the old tech?" Ask, "Does the incumbent have the capital and distribution to absorb the new tech and force-feed it to their existing user base?"
