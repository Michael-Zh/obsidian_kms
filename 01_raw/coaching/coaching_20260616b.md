# Coaching Session — What J Did Right — 2026-06-16

## The Central Observation

J was operating at the political ecology layer. You were operating at the problem-solving layer. Both messages in that chat were about the same issue, but J's had leverage and yours didn't — not because the content was better, but because of how it was framed before the ask arrived.

Her opening message to Yi isn't primarily about the 24-hour display problem. It's a mandate establishment: two roles, IBU representative status, OKR alignment ("exact same target, same as few FBU PMs"), relationships named (Nikki and Serena). By the time she raises the painpoint, she has already answered the implicit question every senior stakeholder asks: *why is this person's problem my problem?* J made the shared KPI explicit before making the ask. That's the difference between a request that creates obligation and a request that can be declined.

## What She Did, Specifically

**1. Mandate-first opening.** J spent three full paragraphs establishing who she is and why she has standing before naming a single ask. Michael's message led with the product observation. J's opened with structural positioning. In matrix orgs — and especially in cross-BU work — nobody acts on a request until they've resolved "is this my problem?" J answered that preemptively.

**2. The shared KPI move.** "One of my OKR also set as upsell %, exact same target, same as few FBU PMs." This is quietly devastating. It collapses the "not my problem" objection before it can form. Shared OKRs mean shared accountability — and saying it out loud forces Yi into a position where declining to engage has a cost too.

**3. Escalation without attack.** When Doris couldn't answer ("I don't know"), J didn't wait. She went to Yi directly — but framed it as relationship-building, not complaint escalation. "I noticed Doris could be junior PM, so I decided to reach out to you directly, in order to establish good work relationships." This is the aikido move: the escalation is visible but wrapped in generosity. It's not "your PM failed me." It's "I want to work with you directly."

**4. A scoped, concrete close.** "All I want to know at this moment is, who has the switch for AB testing and how we can work together." Not open-ended. Not "can we do something about this." One specific question. This is easy to say yes to because it costs very little to answer.

**5. Visual proof at the moment of maximum attention.** The screenshot showing every fare card with identical cancellation policy lands right after the positioning is established. Data as argument, not as background. Michael's message had the same screenshot but it opened cold — without the political scaffolding, it read as a technical observation rather than a business problem.

## Why You Didn't Do This (POS Connection)

The pattern in your POS is "invisible excellence" — the Strategic Friction Rule names it explicitly (1.1 / 2.1). Your message to Doris was correct, analytical, and technically sound. It identified the issue, proposed the hypothesis, framed the experiment. But it didn't name your standing, your OKR stake, or the business cost of delay. You assumed the work would be legible on its own terms. It wasn't.

This is also the "Three-R Practice" you have in the Influence Layer (0.2) described from the outside — lead with conclusions, translate work into business language, circulate with your name attached. J's message to Yi is what Three-R looks like when executed at the stakeholder level. You know this framework. The gap is deploying it in real political moments, not just understanding it analytically.

The "Strategist Instinct Misfired" patch (1.3 Competence Freeze, J-meeting variant) is relevant here: you identified the issue, raised it to a peer (Doris), got a non-answer, and waited rather than escalating. The instinct was correct. The delivery layer stalled. J's move — go to Yi, frame as relationship-building — is the exact externalisation-before-the-internal-loop-runs move the patch is describing.

## The Concrete Moves

For the next stakeholder push in this project (or any cross-BU ask):

1. **Open every message with mandate + shared stake.** Before the observation or ask: who are you, why do you have standing, what's the OKR you share with the person you're asking. Two sentences max. This is not padding — it's what makes the ask land.

2. **"I don't know" from a junior contact = escalation trigger, not waiting signal.** The moment you get that response, your next move is to go to their senior with the same "establish relationship + shared KPI" framing J used. Don't wait to see if Doris follows up.

3. **Make the shared OKR explicit in every cross-BU message.** You have upsell % as a shared target. Say it. It's not aggressive — it's alignment. J said it, and it changed the conversation from request to obligation.

4. **Use a scoped close.** End every stakeholder message with one specific question, not an open invitation. "Who owns the AB test switch?" is answerable. "Can we work together on this?" is not.
