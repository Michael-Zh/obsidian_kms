## Discussion Note — 2026-06-20: Session Architecture

**Question:** Should recurring workflows (wiki-coach, coaching) stay in one long-running session, or start fresh each time? And should mixed topics (Finance + Home) be combined or split?

**On context vs. token cost:** Prompt caching reduces billing for repeated prefixes, but a long accumulated session still loads all prior context into the model's attention window on every turn — which can degrade reasoning quality. The cost to separate is low; the benefit is a cleaner working context.

**Recommended session architecture:**

| Situation | Approach |
|---|---|
|  | New session each run — stateless by design, loads fresh context via skill |
| Finance deep work | New session — focused project context |
| FlyUpsell / project work | New session — keep project context dominant |
| Mixed coaching topics (Finance + Home + Dance in one check-in) | Same session — coaching skill is designed for this; it holds the priority frame and routes each topic |
| Follow-up on a coaching session output | Same session is fine |

**Rule of thumb:** One session = one type of cognitive work. Synthesis/reflection (coaching) is one type. Deep project execution is another. Don't mix them.

# Coaching Session — J's Communication Style — 2026-06-17

## What's Actually Happening in That Meeting

Before naming what's uncomfortable, it's worth naming what's true: J's frustration in this meeting is mostly aimed at the system — the data team transition, Martin's silence, the self-service tool that "launched a very poor product and then forcing us to use it." She says explicitly "I blame you a lot" (to Yian, not Michael), "I'm so mad," and describes an 80-minute complaint session she had with Yian the day before. The emotional register you're absorbing is not coming from you — it's bleed-over from a battle she was already in before the call started.

That said: it lands on you. "I already explained this before. I'm so mad." "No, we cannot look like this." "That's not done, Li." Those are clipped, dismissive in tone, and they happen at moments when you were trying to work through something live. The discomfort you're feeling is real, not oversensitive. The question is whether this is something to fix, something to route around, or something to protect yourself from.

## The J Communication Pattern

J processes in advance. By the time she's in the meeting, she's already verified every hive table, nearly finished the code, and has a fully formed position. What she wants from the meeting is rapid confirmation and role assignment — not collaborative discovery. When you narrate uncertainty out loud ("I'm not entirely sure I understand this correctly," or sharing exploratory data that isn't yet the right shape), it reads to her as not-ready. Not wrong — just not what she's there for. The "I already explained this" reaction is J's frustration that the cognitive gap is larger than she expected, not a critique of your capability.

The hard part: her communication style under frustration — short sentences, topic cuts, "I don't care," redirecting before your thought finishes — is genuinely not helpful for your growth. It doesn't give you the reasoning behind the redirects. It creates pressure to agree rather than understand. Your POS names this exact pattern: INTJ manager, passive-aggression, emotional shield. And the patch is already there: BLUF before updates to J, state opinions clearly even if unsure, and don't narrate the reasoning process — lead with the conclusion, offer to show the data if she wants it.

## What You Did in the Meeting That Invited Friction

Two specific moments created the most visible friction:

1. **Showing exploratory work without a frame.** You opened your data share mid-reasoning ("these are the primary orders... I was trying to see what is the upper limit...") — J cut it: "No, we cannot look like this." This is the Competence Freeze → over-build → show-the-work pattern from your POS (1.3, J-meeting variant). The hypothesis wasn't stated first. The patch: one sentence of hypothesis before you open any data. "My read is that global 24-hour coverage is the bigger risk than void — here's the EY numbers." Then show the data. She can agree, redirect, or add context — but from an anchor, not into a void.

2. **Asking J to teach you her escalation approach mid-frustration.** "How would you usually push this back?" is a legitimate question — but it puts J in mentor mode when she's in execution mode and already frustrated. She reacted: "You ask me. Before, and I explain to you before." This is a meta-question that can be asked outside the meeting, not inside it. Go into the next catchup with a proposed approach already formed ("I'm going to log this in IDEV2 and assign to Martin — does that match your guideline?") — and ask for a redirect if you're wrong, not for instructions.

## Growth Frame vs. Stress Frame

The stress frame: J's communication is creating pressure, sapping your mood, and making it harder to learn. True.

The growth frame: J is one of the fastest-moving people you work with on this project. She had verified every data table and nearly finished the code *overnight*, while you were still working out the conceptual model. That's the gap to close — not the tone. The tone won't change. J is consistently more prepared than the meeting requires; the path to a less pressured dynamic is arriving at the same cognitive level, not asking her to slow down.

This doesn't mean tolerating passive-aggression indefinitely. But the first move is removing the conditions that invite it — which means showing up to J catchups with a formed hypothesis and a status, not with exploratory data and questions. When that gap closes, the meetings get shorter, calmer, and more collaborative.

## Concrete Moves

1. **Pre-meeting hypothesis, one sentence, written down.** Before each J catchup: what do you believe right now, and what's the one thing you need to confirm or redirect? Open with it. Don't open with data.

2. **Status, not process.** Any update to J should be "I did X, I found Y, my next step is Z." Not "I was trying to check this but I wasn't sure, so I looked at..." She doesn't need the path — she needs the current position.

3. **Save the meta-questions for async.** "How should we escalate this?" or "how would you normally handle X?" — send these as a quick message before or after the call. In-call is for confirmations, not instructions.

4. **Separate the frustration from the signal.** When J cuts a line of reasoning ("that's not right"), assume the signal is valid and ask *only* what would make it right. Don't take the cut personally — take the redirect seriously.
