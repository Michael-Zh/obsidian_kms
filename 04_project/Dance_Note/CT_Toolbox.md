# CT Toolbox

Countertechnique personal reference — organized by the six categories of the toolbox. Each entry is a cue, image, or principle. Where a note belongs equally to two categories, the secondary is noted as `[Primary, Secondary]`.

## A — Perspective
*Direction and point of view, especially in relation to the joints*

1. Everything moves away from the ankle: feet travel away, femur travels away — think from the ankle's perspective.
2. Lift the hip off the leg, and the leg off the ankle — each joint holds its own space.
3. Step in: the leg travels over the ankle, not just placing the foot down.
4. All ribs move away from the legs.
5. Nose is not your ribs — they can face different directions independently. `[Perspective, Body]` [[#^ct-c-nose-ribs]] ^ct-a-nose-ribs
6. The pelvis moves away from the heel; the pelvis tips away to the side.
7. Knees move away from each other.
8. Elbows move away from each other — creates more space around you.
9. Keep the front shoulder upward.
10. The heel connects to the last two metatarsals; heel traveling back and down counterbalances something moving upward (shoulders, chest). `[Perspective, Body]` [[#^ct-c-heel-meta]] ^ct-a-heel-meta
11. In side bend: wide back; arm not overhead until the very end — use it to lengthen. Opposite leg presses into the floor to initiate.
12. In forward fold: loosen the upper back to allow the lower back to soften (also lift the toes); the lift initiates from the front of the pelvis.
13. Release the hips so you can actually go into plié. From the release and stretch, you find the power and engine.
14. Think about your mid-back, stretch your mid-back and lift.
15. Bend and lengthen your knees and legs, especially in combinations.
16. Lift the front of your pelvis.
17. Your sitting bone is looking towards the floor.
18. Think about your side body.
19. Be aware that my inner heel is not pressing into the floor, and I'm rolling towards my outer feet.
20. in arm side and up and twist (wind wipe) arms are in front of myself and arms out and up to keep giving more space to lower back
21. in lounge, arm circles, and the front of pelvis and trunk is moving up and my leg lengths so much that i can lift my feet

## C — Body
*Anatomical facts that reframe what you know about your body*

1. Lower back spans from the lower rib to the hip joint — longer than most people assume.
2. The ankle is lower than you think — this creates more space for the foot to bend forward.
3. The heel connects to the last two metatarsals. `[Body, Perspective]` [[#^ct-a-heel-meta]] ^ct-c-heel-meta
4. Nose is not your ribs — they can face different directions. `[Body, Perspective]` [[#^ct-a-nose-ribs]] ^ct-c-nose-ribs
5. Spine has many pieces — it's not one rigid column.
6. In side bend: the top of the spine is higher than you think.
7. Shoulder blades sit on top of the hips, not collapsed forward.
8. The shoulder flows on the muscle and ribcage — think of an oval shape to balance front and back.
9. Hip joint is lower than you think.

## D — Energy
*Physics in relation to gravity; managing tension*

1. Fat feet — let the feet be heavy and full, not tense.
2. Reduce unnecessary tension — around the ankle, eyes, brain, and beyond. → see [[Ballet_Notes#^ballet-pdb-elbow-down]] ^ct-d-reduce-tension
3. Let the energy travel — don't block or get tight, especially in balance.
4. Falling: every part of the body falls, around the joints.
5. Get used to the feeling of falling; stretching out the body helps the fall reach the ground.
6. Directing energy through the floor.
7. Shift the whole body along with the heels.
8. Fall forward: lift the hips, give space to the hips to move and rotate.
9. Soften the jaw and tongue — let them drop to release unnecessary tension in head circles, side folds, and generally. `[Energy, Body]`

## F — CT Principles
*Structural rules of how the method works*

1. Think from the joint, not from the muscle.
2. Feel the main joint before moving.
3. Pick a perspective and a supporting tool in the toolbox to focus on each time — use it as your entry point, not a rule.
4. Sustain it and release it.
5. Distinguish release and relax: release is an active choice; relax is a rest state.
6. Pre-falling exercise — four stages: travel, step, on the leg, fall. Identify the time difference; catch up along the movement, not just at checkpoints — follow the same weight distribution throughout.
7. Because I'm so used to form-based training, whenever someone says to "press and use your power" or "stretch" or "tighten," I tend to tense everything instead of using just the right muscles. 
8. For example, when you say "lengthen," you don't really need to squeeze everything to achieve that. Sometimes you just have to think about it to release unnecessary tension.

22. Bring the front of the pelvis up off the legs — creates availability in the hip joint so the legs can spiral and activate much more easily. (ref: [[2026-06-22]]) [Perspective, Energy]

10. When carrying weight on one shoulder, imagine a diagonal line between the left lower back and the right shoulder lengthening — relieves chronic fatigue/tension in that cross-body diagonal. (ref: [[2026-06-24]]) [Body, Energy]
