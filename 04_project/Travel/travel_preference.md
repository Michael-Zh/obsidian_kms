# Travel Preferences

Use this as the baseline for any trip planning.

---

## Who I Am as a Traveller

Usually a solo traveller (occasionally with partner). Aesthetically driven — sightseeing choices are filtered through a specific taste profile (see [[Aesthetic-Intelligence]]). Balance depth and coverage. Active cultural participant, not just observer — takes ballet/dance classes on the road, attends performances, seeks professional open-class studios comparable to **Henry Jurrien Studio (Amsterdam)** and **Maemero (Berlin)**.

## Decision-Making Process

### 1. Anchor event first
Every trip is built around a primary anchor — a festival, event, or experience that justifies the destination. Everything else is structured around it. The anchor determines arrival day and the most critical scheduling constraints (e.g., parade at 5PM on Day 1 → build backwards).

### 2. Accommodation before activities
Hotel is decided before activities because it determines the energy budget for everything else. Sleep quality is a hard constraint, not a preference.

### 3. Heat/climate structuring
In hot cities (35°C+), the entire day is segmented around temperature peaks. Museums are not optional add-ons — they ARE the afternoon programme (11AM–6PM). No hotel rest during peak heat; use that window for culture. Outdoor and active exploration happens in the morning and after 6PM.

### 4. Recovery phases are planned, not improvised
After high-stimulus events (e.g., Pride parade + nightlife), a structured recovery phase follows — lighter schedule, earlier sleep, deliberate decompression. This is protocol, not weakness.

### 5. Research attractions before finalising the schedule
Before committing to any attraction or museum, verify three things:
- **Opening hours and days** — many major museums close one day a week (e.g. Reina Sofía closed Tuesdays, closes 2:30PM on Sundays). Check the official site, not aggregate travel content.
- **Special events or temporary exhibitions** worth prioritising — a temporary show can make the visit significantly better and may need a separate ticket.
- **Free/reduced rate access windows** — numerous museums offer free entry at specific times (e.g. Thyssen permanent collection free Mondays 12–4PM; Reina Sofía free 7–9PM on selected days). These can reshape the itinerary and save €30–50 per trip.

**Museum pass vs. free slots:** Don't assume a city pass is the default. Map the free windows first, then calculate whether a pass actually saves money. The free slot approach often wins and has the added benefit of restructuring the day around better timing (e.g. evening visit when crowds thin).

### 6. Day trips for architectural contrast
Short high-speed rail day trips (30–90 min) are a reliable format for breaking the frame — high architectural contrast with low logistical friction. Toledo and Segovia from Madrid are the canonical example. Depart by 9AM to get 4–5 hours before afternoon heat.

**When to plan multiple day trips:** If the stay is 6+ days and the base city is a strong hub (e.g. Madrid, Paris, Rome), plan 2–3 day trip options ranked by distance, architectural contrast, and effort. Put the hardest trip early when energy is highest. Keep a Parking Lot of shortlisted-but-unscheduled day trips — useful if a free day opens up unexpectedly.

## Accommodation

### Hard requirements
- **Quiet room** — high floor, interior courtyard-facing if possible. Explicitly request in writing when booking.
- **Air conditioning** — non-negotiable in summer destinations.
- **No smoking** — confirmed non-smoking room.

### Neighborhood logic
- **Never the epicenter.** During events like Pride, staying in the noise-radius neighbourhood carries a high physiological cost. Prefer a peripheral, well-connected neighbourhood 1–2 Metro stops away.
- Good Madrid model: Chamberí or Retiro over Chueca. Quiet residential neighbourhood, Metro-connected to the action.
- Salamanca (Madrid) is an alternative — more upscale, calmer, slightly further.

### Budget
- Target: **€100–130/night**. Will exceed for significant soundproofing or design quality justification.
- Split hotel (noisy zone for event nights → quiet zone for recovery) is on the table if it makes logistical sense, but a single quiet hotel throughout is preferred when budget allows.

### Accommodation — what to avoid
From the Madrid shortlisting process, these hotel profiles consistently fail the criteria:
- **Industrial park / business park hotels** (e.g. Mercamadrid, Las Tablas, IFEMA area) — 10km+ from centre, designed for trade fair travellers, wrong for a city trip.
- **Peripheral suburban hotels** (Móstoles, Las Rozas, Alcalá) — only viable if the trip's anchor is in that direction; never for a city-centre programme.
- **Budget-brand chains in central locations** (Ibis, etc.) — weak soundproofing as a category; noise bleeds between rooms.
- **Tourist epicentre locations** (Puerta del Sol, Chueca) — high street and internal corridor noise; unsustainable for a full week regardless of room quality.
- **Any hotel with flagged A/C reliability issues** in July reviews — opening windows to compensate introduces street noise; worst of both worlds.


- Book immediately once dates are set — event-week hotels sell out fast.
- Always confirm: bed configuration (double, not two singles), floor level, and room orientation in the booking notes.

### Breakfast decision
When booking, always research and make an explicit call on breakfast — don't default to the hotel option without checking.

**Check hotel breakfast first:**
- Read recent reviews specifically mentioning breakfast (quality, variety, freshness). A buffet with poor reviews is not worth the premium.
- Note the price: hotel breakfast in Europe typically runs €15–25/person. Compare against local alternatives.

**Then consider local breakfast culture:**
- Some destinations have a strong standalone breakfast scene worth doing instead — Spanish *tostada con tomate* + coffee at a neighbourhood bar (~€3–5) is better and cheaper than a hotel buffet. Same logic applies in Italy (bar cornetto), France (boulangerie), etc.
- In destinations without a distinct breakfast culture (Northern Europe, US), hotel breakfast is more defensible.

**Factor in your schedule:**
- Early departure days (ballet class, long day trip): hotel breakfast is faster and avoids a separate errand.
- Slow mornings: a neighbourhood café is more pleasant and gives 15 min of street-level orientation.

**Default recommendation:** If hotel breakfast reviews are mediocre or the price exceeds €18, skip it and find the nearest local bar/café. If reviews are strong and the timing is tight, it's worth including.

## Aesthetic Profile → Sightseeing Filter

Full detail in [[Aesthetic-Intelligence]]. Condensed travel-relevant version:

**Strongly drawn to:**
- Gothic and Islamic architecture; medieval layering; three-religion urban fabric (Toledo is the model)
- Art Nouveau and Art Deco — architecture and applied design
- Brutalism and adaptive reuse (Matadero Madrid model)
- Constructivism and geometric abstraction (Mondrian, Reina Sofía)
- Dutch Golden Age painting — Rembrandt, Vermeer
- Impressionist
- Spanish masters in situ — Velázquez, Bosch
- Contemporary dance and physical theatre — European choreographic work, especially non-central-European circuits
- Parametric and structural architecture — Zaha Hadid, Gaudí

**Museum sequencing principle:** One major museum per afternoon session (2–3h max). A lighter monument or site (e.g. Royal Palace, ~2h) can precede a museum on the same day if scheduled in the morning before heat peaks.

**What to skip:** Tourist-circuit repeats. If I've already done the landmark (e.g., Sagrada Família, Uffizi), recommend the less-obvious adjacent experience instead.

## LGBTQ+ Infrastructure

An active dimension of trip planning, not an afterthought.

- **Gay neighbourhood** is a reference point for bars, nightlife, and local scene — Chueca (Madrid), Marais (Paris), Gaixample (Barcelona), Schöneberg (Berlin).
- **Gay saunas** are a genuine activity to plan for — best visited on weekday afternoons (quieter, post-heat window) or after midnight on weekends during events.
- **Pride events** — if the destination has Pride during travel dates, treat it as an anchor event with full schedule implications (parade route, closing ceremony, nightlife, crowd management).
- **Drag shows** — not really into drag shows.

## Performing Arts & Dance

### Performances to seek
- National ballet companies — look for world premieres or new productions; avoid touring productions I might see at home.
- Contemporary dance — physical theatre, European choreographic structures outside the central circuit.

### Classes
- Drop-in professional-level ballet or contemporary classes are a travel routine.
- Reference studios: **Henry Jurrien Studio (Amsterdam)**, **Maemero (Berlin)** — professional-track adult classes, floor work/floor barre
- In a new city: look for studios offering intermediate/advanced adult drop-in. English-taught is a bonus but not required.
- Ideal scheduling: morning class (9–11AM) before the heat builds, on a non-heavy-sightseeing day. or usual after working hour (6-7PM) after the sightseeing.

## Food & Dining

- **Lunch:** 2–4PM local schedule. Dinner not before 9PM in Spain. Don't fight the local rhythm.
- **Pre-plan every meal slot** — lunch and dinner for each day are drafted when building the itinerary, keyed to the nearest sightseeing location. Don't leave meals as "figure it out on the day" — heat and crowds make last-minute decisions costly.
- **Default format:** Tapas bar-hopping for dinner — no reservations needed, walk and pick. La Latina (Madrid) is the reference neighbourhood.
- **For family/group dinners:** Book well in advance. Prefer historic or institutionally significant restaurants that work across generations without requiring food adventurousness.
- **Markets** for casual lunch: Mercado de San Miguel (Madrid). Quick, standing, no commitment.

### Restaurant research checklist
Before adding any restaurant to an itinerary:
1. **Verify still open** — search for current status before recommending. Many well-known restaurants close permanently without notice; do not rely on older sources.
2. **Verify open on the specific day and time** — check opening hours for the exact day of the week the visit falls on. Many restaurants are closed one day per week, or have different lunch/dinner hours than assumed.
3. **Budget cap: €40/person including a drink** — no fine dining or tasting menus. Target is good neighbourhood restaurants, casual bistros, tapas bars, and market spots. The food should be the point, not the price tag.

## Transport — In-City Planning

### Mode hierarchy
1. **Walk first** — before defaulting to metro, check whether the distance is ≤25 min on foot. Many itineraries are far more walkable than they appear on a map, especially when sightseeing is clustered in a museum district or residential neighbourhood. Walking is free, removes transit friction, and surfaces the city better.
2. **Metro** for distances over 25 min or when heat makes walking unreasonable.
3. **Taxi/Uber** for: airport with luggage, late night, cross-city trips to out-of-centre destinations (e.g. parents in a business park), and any leg where a metro transfer makes the journey awkward.

### Ticket recommendation process
Don't assume a tourist pass is the right choice. Calculate first:

1. **Map the actual metro trips** needed for the itinerary — day by day, accounting for which legs are walkable. The real number is almost always lower than the headline number of "days".
2. **Check if contactless bank card payment is available** — many European metro systems (Madrid, London, Amsterdam, Paris) now accept Visa/Mastercard tap-to-pay at turnstiles. If so, no card purchase needed; pay per trip.
3. **Compare cost: per-trip vs. pass** — a tourist pass only wins if you'd take enough trips to break even. Example Madrid calc: 5-day Zone A pass ~€26.80 / single trip ~€1.50 = needs 18 trips to break even. Most cultural itineraries don't hit that.
4. **Airport is usually a separate zone** — verify whether the unlimited pass covers airport lines or requires a supplement. If not covered, taxi to/from airport may be simpler with luggage.

### Preferred format for transport recommendation in the itinerary
For each day: note which legs are walkable (with approximate walk time), which need metro (with line number), and any taxi legs. Then give a single ticket recommendation at the end with the break-even logic made explicit.

## Links Convention

All venues in any itinerary document must carry two links:
- **Official website** — for booking, hours, and ticket info
- **Google Maps link** — `[Maps](https://maps.google.com/?q=VENUE+NAME+CITY)` format, Obsidian-compatible inline link

Both are added at the point where the venue first appears (attraction, restaurant, studio, transport hub). This makes every location directly clickable from the plan.

## Planning Checklist Template

For any new trip, resolve these in order. **Open Questions go at the top of every trip plan** — unresolved decisions should be visible immediately on every read.

1. [ ] Anchor event identified and dated
2. [ ] Flight/train booked
3. [ ] Hotel booked (quiet neighbourhood, high floor, interior room requested)
4. [ ] Performance/dance programme researched (national company, contemporary venues)
5. [ ] Museum shortlist filtered against aesthetic profile (max 3, one per afternoon)
   - For each museum: verify opening hours, closed days, free access windows, and any temp exhibitions worth prioritising
6. [ ] Ballet/dance class drop-in options identified
7. [ ] Day trip destination selected and train looked up
8. [ ] LGBTQ+ infrastructure mapped (neighbourhood, saunas, events)
9. [ ] All venues given official website link + Google Maps link
10. [ ] Lunch and dinner pre-planned for every day, keyed to sightseeing location
11. [ ] Family dinner booked if applicable
12. [ ] Transport plan: identify walkable legs, metro lines needed, ticket type recommended (with break-even calc)
13. [ ] Heat/climate protocol applied to daily schedule
13. [ ] Google Calendar sync script updated and run once itinerary is finalised
14. [ ] Parking Lot created for anything that doesn't fit — don't discard, just defer
