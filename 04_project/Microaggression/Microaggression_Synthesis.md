## 1. What Is Microaggression / Discrimination?

**Microaggressions** are brief, everyday exchanges that communicate negative or demeaning messages to members of a marginalized group — often unintentionally, and often by well-meaning individuals. The term was coined by Chester Pierce (1970s) and later expanded by Derald Wing Sue.

**Three forms:**
- **Microinsults** — communications that convey rudeness or insensitivity and demean a person's identity (e.g., "cheap things from China")
- **Microinvalidations** — communications that exclude, negate, or nullify experiences ("you're overreacting")
- **Microassaults** — more conscious, deliberate attacks closer to overt discrimination (e.g., targeted street harassment)

**Discrimination** is the systemic or institutional level: structural disadvantage embedded in hiring, promotion, pay, or social norms that operates even without individual intent.

**The "model minority" trap** is a specific discrimination mechanism. It presents a marginalized group (Asians, in Western contexts) as high-achieving and compliant — which simultaneously (a) suppresses wages and career advancement by making "flawless execution" invisible, (b) invalidates claims of discrimination ("you're doing fine"), and (c) creates social targeting because the group is perceived as low-risk to harass.

**Key insight:** Harassers operate on a zero-risk calculation. Microaggressions persist because they carry low social cost for the perpetrator. The system quietly sanctions them, leaving targets to absorb the impact alone.

## 2. What It Looks Like / How to Identify It

### Street-Level
- **Physical intimidation:** Teenagers on bikes trying to scare, cut off, or crowd an Asian person; children using water guns as "harmless" targeted harassment
- **Verbal slurs or degrading references:** "cheap things from China" used as an insult, not a factual statement
- **Social invisibility:** Being talked around, skipped in conversation, assumed to speak no Dutch/English

**How to recognize:** Ask — is this happening because of my perceived identity? Would this happen to a white person in the same situation? The test is not whether the person "meant it" — intent is irrelevant to impact.

### Professional / Corporate
- **Visibility suppression:** High-quality work attributed to the team, not you; your strategy becomes "the plan" without your name
- **Scope narrowing:** Being directed toward execution tasks while peers are invited into strategic discussions
- **Tone policing:** Being told you're "too direct," "too quiet," or need to "work on your presence"
- **The excellence trap:** Rewarded for doing more, not for leading more — Jason vs. Priya (see Case Studies)

**The underlying logic:** In European corporate environments, the "model minority" stereotype quietly sanctions treating the Asian colleague as capable infrastructure, not leadership material.

### Identity Politics / Solidarity Gaps
- **Absent empathy from other marginalized groups:** Oppression generates lateral violence, not spontaneous solidarity. Other marginalized groups may target Asians precisely because Asians are perceived as low-retaliation, high-status targets — a pressure-release valve.
- **Allyship as interest calculation:** True solidarity is interest-based, not identity-based. Feeling disconnected from other groups' causes (e.g., Keti Koti) when you're actively defending your own dignity is not a moral failing — it reflects bandwidth constraints.

## 3. Tactics and Options by Scenario and Condition

### 3A. Street Harassment — Breaking the Frame

The goal: alter the harasser's risk/reward calculation without physical escalation. The key move is removing the expected response (fear, avoidance, silence).

| Tactic | What You Do | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Status Inversion (Pity over Anger)** | Deny them the status of threat. Reframe them as clumsy children. E.g., calmly: *"Voorzichtig, je valt bijna."* | When harassment is performative — they want fear or anger |
| **Static Wall (Physical Disruption)** | Stop moving. Drop both feet. Stand upright. Stare blankly. Shift the burden of navigation onto them entirely. | When they're cutting you off, trying to crowd, or intimidate through movement |
| **Cognitive Short-Circuit (Aggressive Cheerfulness)** | React with a non-sequitur. Smile and wave enthusiastically. *"Lekker weertje hé!"* | When you want confusion, not confrontation; low-energy, high-disruption |
| **Surveillance Variable (Remove Anonymity)** | Hold up your phone horizontally as if recording. Spikes perceived risk immediately. | When you want them to back off without any verbal exchange |

**Underlying logic:** Each tactic introduces unpredictable friction into the zero-risk calculation. You're not fighting back — you're removing the reward. The harasser expected a particular response; giving them nothing, or giving them something strange, collapses the script.

**Condition modifiers:**
- Group vs. solo: Static Wall works better solo; Short-Circuit works better in groups (more audience)
- Safety check first: if there's any physical risk, Surveillance Variable is safest (no confrontation)
- Energy state: Status Inversion requires calm; if you're activated, Short-Circuit requires less regulation

### 3B. Professional — Dismantling the Model Minority Trap

**Core principle:** The trap is maintained by your own flawless compliance. Strategic friction is the counter-move.

| Tactic | What You Do | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **"No" with a Thesis (Weaponize Data)** | Decline minor tasks by pivoting to macro strategy: *"I'm deprioritizing this metric adjustment because the core vulnerability in our upsell conversion is X."* | When you're being funneled toward execution work |
| **Upgrade the Currency (1-on-1 Reframe)** | Change 1-on-1 agendas from status updates to risk analyses and bold projections. Force peer-level strategic debate. | When you're treated as a reporter, not a peer |
| **Recalibrate Peer Leverage** | Clearly delineate your strategic design from others' operational execution. Retain visibility for architecture decisions. | When your work gets absorbed into the team's output without credit |
| **Lead with Conclusions (3R Model)** | State the recommendation first, not the analysis. *"I don't know, but based on what I do know..."* — own the judgment, not just the data. | Consistently — this is the Priya vs. Jason distinction |
| **Translate to Business Language** | Convert technical/analytical work into stakeholder language: not "model accuracy improved 4%" but "customer complaints projected to drop 12%" | In cross-functional meetings, performance reviews, exec updates |
| **Circulate Your Work** | After each significant project, write a brief retrospective email. Let results speak with your name attached. | End of each project cycle |

**The underlying logic:** You are not making yourself more likable — you are restructuring your relationship with the organization so your strategic thinking is structurally visible, not just your output.

### 3C. Internal Processing — Identity and Solidarity

- **Your romantic preferences are yours.** They are hard data about your subjective experience. You can intellectually acknowledge that global desirability standards are Eurocentric without owing your romantic energy as political equity.
- **You do not owe solidarity bandwidth** you don't have. Defending your own dignity uses resources. Feeling disconnected from another group's cause when you're under attack is self-preservation, not betrayal.
- **Anger is valid data.** The instinct to de-escalate is protective — but the anger underneath it is accurate signal. Use it as a compass, not a liability.

## 4. Case Studies

### Case 1: Fat Bike Street Harassment (Amsterdam, NL, 2026)
**Context:** Teenagers on bikes targeting an Asian man cycling in Amsterdam.  
**Type:** Microassault, street-level, group/public setting  
**Pattern:** Low perceived risk for harassers (Asian = polite, avoidant, won't retaliate); group emboldening  
**Response available:** Static Wall (stop, drop feet, stare) or Surveillance Variable (phone up)  
**Analysis:** Classic zero-risk harassment. The goal is to introduce cost — either through disruption (Static Wall breaks their momentum) or perceived risk (Surveillance Variable makes them think twice about their identities being captured).  
**Source:** [[Strategic_Navigation_NL_Export]]

### Case 2: "Cheap Things from China" (Amsterdam, NL, 2026)
**Context:** Casual comment used as a degrading reference, not a factual statement  
**Type:** Microinsult, verbal, casual/social setting  
**Pattern:** Anti-Asian economic stereotype weaponized as insult  
**Response available:** Status Inversion (reframe the person as clumsy/ignorant rather than engaging the insult directly); alternatively, Short-Circuit (cheerful non-response that denies them the status of having landed a hit)  
**Analysis:** The insult operates by making you absorb the degradation. Refusing to engage with its premise — while remaining visibly unaffected — removes the reward.  
**Source:** [[Strategic_Navigation_NL_Export]]

### Case 3: Jason vs. Priya — The Technician vs. Strategist Trap (Corporate, 2026)
**Context:** Asian engineer at a Western company; talent review reveals technical excellence ≠ advancement  
**Type:** Systemic discrimination via model minority mechanism; professional setting  
**Pattern:** "Jason把事做到了极致。Priya让周围人变得更好" — flawless execution read as infrastructure, not leadership  
**Response available:** 3R model (conclusion-first communication), business language translation, proactive output circulation  
**Analysis:** The trap is the assumption that technical excellence speaks for itself. In Western corporate culture, influence is performed — you build it through proactive communication acts. Jason waited 3 years; Priya built it incrementally. The fix is structural: change the visibility architecture of your work, not the quality.  
**Source:** [[洋人公司里爬得最快的亚裔，都有同一张脸]]
